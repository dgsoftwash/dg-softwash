📋 Forms & Documents Guide
VA Form 22-1990e — Transfer of Entitlement

What you need before you start

  • Your VA file number or sponsor's Social Security Number
  • Sponsor's branch of service and discharge date (from DD-214)
  • School name, address, and VA certifying official contact
  • Intended major / program of education
  • Anticipated start date

Step-by-step

  • Find your VA file number: Log in to VA.gov → My VA → look for "VA file number" in your profile, or call 1-800-827-1000. It's often your SSN but may be different for older filers.
  • Find your school's VA certifying official: Call the financial aid or registrar's office and ask specifically for the "VA certifying official" or "SCO." They handle enrollment certification and can confirm the school is approved.
  • "Program of education" just means your degree/certificate program (e.g., "B.S. in Computer Science"). Use the catalog name from the school's website.
  • Apply at: va.gov/education/apply-for-education-benefits/application/1990E/ — processing typically takes 4–6 weeks. Apply as early as possible before the semester starts.
Once approved, you'll get a Certificate of Eligibility (COE). Keep this — you'll need it for Yellow Ribbon and other VA programs.
FAFSA — Free Application for Federal Student Aid

What you need before you start

  • Student's Social Security Number
  • Prior-year federal tax return (use IRS Data Retrieval Tool for auto-fill)
  • Bank account and investment totals (as of the day you file)
  • VA benefit award letter (shows monthly benefit amount)
  • FSA ID (username/password) — create at StudentAid.gov before you sit down

Key things to know

  • Dependency questions: If you are claimed as a dependent on your parents' taxes, you'll need their financial info too. Most students under 24 are dependent unless they meet specific independence criteria.
  • VA benefits are NOT counted as income for the Pell Grant calculation. This is a common misconception — fill out FAFSA even if you receive GI Bill benefits, because you may still qualify for Pell Grants and state aid.
  • Opens October 1 every year for the following academic year. File as early as possible — some state aid (like VGAP) is first-come, first-served.
  • After filing, you'll get a Student Aid Report (SAR) — keep a copy, many scholarship applications require it.
File every year even after freshman year. Aid packages change based on your family's financial situation.
Yellow Ribbon Program

What you need before you start

  • Certificate of Eligibility (COE) from VA — received after approving your 22-1990e
  • School's Yellow Ribbon application form (each school has their own)
  • Copy of 22-1990e approval letter

Step-by-step

  • Confirm 100% entitlement: Yellow Ribbon is only available to veterans (not dependents using transferred benefit) with 100% Post-9/11 GI Bill eligibility. Verify your COE shows 100%.
  • Request your COE online: After submitting Form 22-1990e, your COE arrives by mail or you can download it from VA.gov → Education Benefits. Call 1-888-442-4551 if you haven't received it within 6 weeks.
  • Find the school's financial aid YR contact: Search "[School Name] Yellow Ribbon Program" or call their financial aid office and ask for the Yellow Ribbon coordinator.
  • Apply early — seats are limited: Schools set their own caps on how many students they accept each year. Some schools fill quickly. Apply as soon as you have your COE.
Not all schools participate, and those that do may have seat limits. Check the VA's Yellow Ribbon school search tool at va.gov before committing to a school.
General Scholarship Applications — Standard Checklist

Documents to prepare

  • Official transcripts — request from your high school registrar at least 2 weeks before deadlines. Some scholarships need official sealed copies mailed directly.
  • 2 recommendation letters — ask teachers, coaches, or counselors at least 6 weeks before the deadline. Give them a brief note about the scholarship and your goals to make their letter stronger.
  • Personal essay — most scholarships ask for 200–500 words. Common prompts: "Why do you deserve this scholarship?" / "How has military service affected your family?" / "Describe your career goals." Draft early and have someone proofread.
  • FAFSA Student Aid Report (SAR) — downloaded from StudentAid.gov after filing FAFSA. Many scholarships require it as proof of financial need.
  • Photo ID — driver's license or school ID. Some applications require a copy for identity verification.
  • Proof of military affiliation (for military scholarships) — copy of sponsor's DD-214 or a military ID card copy.

Tips

  • Keep a folder (digital or physical) with all your documents so you can reuse them across multiple applications.
  • Treat each application like a job application — tailor your essay to the specific scholarship's mission.
  • Apply for everything you qualify for, even small awards. $500–$1,000 scholarships add up and are less competitive.
